The complete fishing education.

From a size-22 dry on a 2-weight in a mountain creek to a 15-weight for marlin — and every spinning reel, soft plastic and Texas rig in between. Start wherever you are.

Where to begin

Fishing splits into two big families of tackle. Fly fishing casts a weighted line to deliver a nearly weightless fly — it's about imitation and presentation. Spin & baitcast fishing casts a weighted lure or bait on thin line — it's faster to learn and covers water quickly. Neither is "better"; they're different tools. Most well-rounded anglers do both.

New & want it easy?

Start with a spinning rod, 6–8 lb mono, and a handful of lures. You'll be catching panfish and bass in an afternoon. Jump to Spin & Baitcast 101.

Drawn to trout & rivers?

Start fly fishing with a 9' 5-weight outfit — the do-everything trout rod. Learn two casts and two knots and you're fishing. Jump to Fly Fishing 101.

The single best free curriculum to pair with this page is the Orvis Learning Center (video lessons, in order) and, for spin/bass, BassResource.

Fly Fishing 101

In fly fishing the line is the weight. The fly is almost weightless, so you load a flexible rod with a thick, tapered fly line and "cast the line," letting the fly go along for the ride. That's the whole trick — and why fly casting feels different from spin casting.

The four pieces of the system

  • Rod — rated by "weight" (1–15, see progression). Higher number = heavier line = bigger fish/flies/wind.
  • Reel — mostly line storage on trout rods; a real drag matters for big fish (salmon, bonefish, tarpon).
  • Fly line — the tapered, coated casting weight. Weight-forward floating (WF-F) covers ~90% of fishing. Sink-tips for streamers/depth.
  • Leader & tippet — a tapered clear monofilament/fluoro section from the thick line down to a fine tippet the fly ties to. Tippet is sized by "X" — bigger X = thinner (see rigging).
The bare-minimum kit: rod, reel, line, leader, a spool or two of tippet, nippers (snips), forceps (hemostats), floatant, and a small box of flies. That's it.
— per the Orvis Learning Center.

The two casts to learn first

The overhead cast (pick up, crisp stop at 10 and 2 o'clock, let the line straighten) and the roll cast (for when there's no room behind you). Tight, abrupt stops form tight loops; "lawn-casting" with no fly for 20 minutes teaches more than anything.

Entomology & matching the hatch

Trout eat mostly bugs, so trout anglers learn the four orders that dominate cold water and how to imitate each life stage. "Match the hatch" means picking a fly that mimics the size, shape, color and stage the fish are eating right now.

InsectLife cycle / stages to imitateWhen
MayflyNymph → emerger → dun (sub-imago) → spinner (imago). The classic dry-fly bug; the most diverse.Spring–fall; species-specific (BWO cool days, PMD/sulphur late spring–summer, Trico late summer mornings)
CaddisflyLarva → pupa → adult (complete metamorphosis). Skittering, tent-wing adults.Late April–Oct; famous October Caddis in fall
StoneflyNymph → adult. The biggest bugs; nymphs crawl to shore to hatch on rocks.Early-season "big bug" nymphing; salmonfly/golden stone hatches late spring
MidgeLarva → pupa → adult. Tiny (#20–28) — the year-round staple, often the only food on winter tailwaters.Year-round; critical winter & tailwater
TerrestrialsLand bugs that fall in: ants, beetles, hoppers, crickets.Summer, especially windy afternoons
Field shortcut: turn over a few rocks and seine the water. Whatever's crawling on the rocks (and what's in the air) tells you the size & color to tie on. When in doubt in winter, go small with a midge; in summer, try a terrestrial.
— see Take Me Fishing & The Catch & The Hatch.

Be water-specific (don't assume): bugs are local. Scuds & sowbugs dominate cold tailwaters (the White, Taneycomo, Norfork); freestone mountain streams lean on caddis, terrestrials & small mayflies; big Western rivers get salmonflies. Types & styles of fly fishing

Dry fly

Floating fly on top, imitating an adult insect. The visual, classic style — you see the eat.

Nymphing

Subsurface, where trout feed 80% of the time. Variants: indicator (bobber) nymphing, Euro/tight-line nymphing (no indicator, heavy jig nymphs), high-stick.

Dry-dropper

A buoyant dry with a nymph hung below — searches two columns at once. Deadly all-rounder.

Streamer

Stripping baitfish/leech imitations to trigger predators. Big-fish tactic; sink-tips & 6–8wt.

Wet fly / swing

Soft-hackles fished down-and-across on the swing — old-school, effective during emergences.

Spey / two-hand

Long rods swinging flies for steelhead & salmon on big rivers.

Tenkara

Fixed-line, no reel — simple Japanese method for small mountain streams.

Warmwater

Poppers & big flies for bass, bluegill, pike & muskie on 6–10wt.

Saltwater

Sight-fishing flats (bonefish, permit, redfish, tarpon) and bluewater (sailfish, tuna). 8–15wt.

Regional styles across the country

The same sport looks different by region: Western big-water dry-dropper & streamer fishing and salmonfly hatches; Appalachian/Southeast small-stream wild-trout dries & nymphs in tight cover; Midwest/Driftless spring-creek technical nymphing; Northeast classic hatch-matching & big browns; Gulf/Southeast flats redfish & tarpon; Florida Keys bonefish/permit/tarpon "grand slam"; Pacific NW steelhead spey; Mountain West tailwaters midge & scud micro-nymphing.

Color, size & selection

Three quick rules cover most decisions:

  • Size first, then shape, then color. Trout key on silhouette and size more than exact color — get those right before fussing over shade.
  • Water clarity drives color. Clear water & bright light → natural, drab, smaller (olive, gray, tan). Stained/muddy water or low light → dark or bright & bigger (black, chartreuse, white) so fish can find it.
  • "Match the hatch" on trout; "match the bait" on predators. For bass/stripers/salt, imitate the dominant forage (shad, mullet, crawfish) in size & color.

Spinning vs. baitcaster

ReelLearning curveBest for
SpinningEasy — just release the line on the forward cast. Fewer tangles.Beginners, light lures & line, finesse, panfish/trout/light bass

Line: mono vs. fluoro vs. braid

LineFeelUse it for
MonofilamentCheap, stretchy, floats, beginner-friendly, easy knotsTopwater, beginners, general use; 6–12 lb
FluorocarbonNearly invisible, sinks, low stretch = sensitiveClear water, bottom baits, leaders; 8–15 lb
BraidSuper strong & thin, no stretch, no memory, floatsHeavy cover, finesse main line (add a fluoro leader); 10–30 lb

Great starter combo: 7' medium spinning rod + 2500–3000 reel, spooled with 10–15 lb braid and a 6–10 lb fluorocarbon leader (or just 8 lb mono to keep it simple).

Leader & tippet "X" sizing (fly)

Tippet is rated in "X" — the bigger the number, the thinner: 0X (~15 lb, big streamers/bass) → 3X–4X (nymphs, hoppers, average trout) → 5X–6X (dries, wary trout) → 7X–8X (tiny midges, spring creeks). Rough rule: divide the fly size by 3 or 4 for the X (a #16 fly ≈ 4–5X).

Glossary

Fly fishing

Tippet — fine end of the leader. Dun/Spinner — mayfly adult stages. Emerger — insect rising to hatch. Dead-drift — fly moving at current speed, no drag. Mend — flipping line upstream to prevent drag. Dropper — second fly below the first. Indicator — strike bobber. Hatch — synchronized insect emergence. Streamer — baitfish/leech fly. Backing — line behind the fly line. WF-F — weight-forward floating line.

Spin / bait

Backlash — baitcaster spool overrun ("bird's nest"). Drag — slip system that tires fish. Leader — abrasion/stealth section at the end. Jighead — weighted hook for plastics. Weedless — hook point hidden to dodge snags. Finesse — light line, small baits, subtle. Flipping/pitching — short accurate close-cover casts. Topwater — surface lure. Circle hook — self-setting hook for bait. Texas/Carolina/Ned/drop-shot — soft-plastic rigs.
